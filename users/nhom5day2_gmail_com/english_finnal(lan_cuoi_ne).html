🏛️ Nghe An 1986
Echoes of the Past

Nghe An 40 Years Ago

Hardship & Resilience

01
Historical Context

End of the Subsidy Period, Dawn of Renovation

The year 1986 marked a historical turning point with the 6th National Party Congress - the beginning of "Đổi Mới" (Renovation). Nghe An at this time belonged to Nghe Tinh province (merged with Ha Tinh from 1976 to 1991).

Nghe Tinh Province
Nghe An and Ha Tinh merged into Nghe Tinh province (1976-1991). This period marked many administrative and economic development difficulties.
Agricultural Economy
Mainly farming, manual labor, low productivity. Cooperatives were the common form of production organization, with equal distribution.
Harsh Foehn Wind (Lao Wind)
Scorching Foehn wind, severe drought from April to August. Frequent natural disasters and floods. The harsh nature forged the resilient character of the people.
02
Transportation

Walking & Bicycles

While the world already had popular cars and motorbikes, Nghe An still relied on bare feet and rusty bicycles.

Walking 5-10km to school
Students crossed fields and waded through streams every day, barefoot or in torn sandals. Waking up at 5 AM, walking 1-2 hours just to get to class. After returning home, they had to help their families farm and herd buffaloes.
Thong Nhat & Phuong Hoang Bicycles
The family's biggest asset, costing several months' salary. The chain often slipped, and flat tires had to be temporarily stuffed with straw/dry grass. One bicycle served a whole family of 3-4 generations.
Motorbikes: Luxury items
Simson and Honda Cub 50 were only owned by the extremely wealthy or officials returning from abroad. A motorbike cost a year's salary and was the pride of the whole neighborhood.
Red dirt roads
Blinding dust on sunny days, turning people red. On rainy days, it was muddy; vehicles got stuck and had to be pushed. No asphalt or concrete yet - village roads were just thick red dirt and jagged rocks.
03
Family Life

No Electricity, No Tap Water

A simple life, close to nature but full of hardships. Everything was done by hand, from cooking to washing.

Electrical Appliances: Almost Zero
No color TVs, no refrigerators, no washing machines, no internet. Only the "fancy" families had battery-powered radio cassettes to listen to news and revolutionary music. Batteries were expensive and had to be saved by the hour.
Three-legged tripod stove
Cooking with straw, stubble, dry firewood, and leaves. Flying ash caused stinging eyes and dry coughs. Rice was mixed with sweet potatoes and cassava because of rice shortages. Had to eat sweet potatoes and cassava to get full; rice was saved for adults doing farm work.
Kerosene lamps (Hoa Ky lamps)
Dim, pale yellow light. At night, the whole family gathered around the small lamp. Those were precious moments: telling stories, studying, repairing fishing nets. Oil was expensive, so lamps had to be turned off early to save it.

At night, the whole family gathered around the small kerosene lamp. That was the most beautiful moment of the day; though the light was dim, family love was overflowing.

04
Community Services

Ward Loudspeakers & Open Wells

No power grid, tap water, or internet. Information was transmitted through ward loudspeakers, and water was drawn from open wells.

Electricity: None or flickering
Rural areas were completely pitch black at night, with only moonlight and starlight. Vinh city had electricity but with constant rolling blackouts: 2 hours in the afternoon, 3 hours at night. When the power came back on, the whole neighborhood celebrated.
Water: Open wells & rainwater
No tap water. Digging wells 10-15m deep or catching rainwater from the roof into earthen jars. Bathing in rivers and streams. Washing clothes by the pond or riverbank. During the dry season, people had to queue for water from early morning.
Ward loudspeakers: The "social network" of that time
At 6 AM and 5 PM, loudspeakers broadcast news for the whole village. Meeting announcements, international news, praises, and criticisms. Loudspeakers were the only source of information; everyone had to listen carefully.
Outdoor movies & storytelling
The communal house courtyard or cooperative yard screened outdoor movies every few months. The whole village flocked to watch, bringing chairs and mats. The movies were usually war films or Soviet films. It was a festival for the whole region.
05
Education & Games

Old Books, Dip Pens, Clay Marbles

Studying in deprived conditions but with passion. Games were simple but joyful and innocent.

Hand-me-down textbooks
Tattered, scribbled all over; older siblings passed them down to younger ones. Purple ink written with dip pens; paper turned dark and dull due to mold. A book had to be used for 5-10 years, patched over and over to continue studying.
Parachute cloth bags, woven sack bags
Self-sewn or sewn by mothers from old parachute cloth or woven sacks. No leather backpacks like today. Torn bags were patched and used until they couldn't be patched anymore.
Folk games
Shooting clay marbles, jumping rubber bands, playing jacks, hide-and-seek, playing coin toss, seahorse chess... No online games, phones, or computers. Simple but fun games that created a memorable childhood.

BUT!

A realistic view of life back then

🚸

Not everyone got to go to school

Many kids the same age (9th grade) had to drop out to herd buffaloes, cut grass, and farm to help their starving families. The dropout rate was very high, especially among schoolgirls. Going to school was a privilege, not a right for everyone.

🦠

Poor hygiene, many diseases

No clean flush toilets like today. Tapeworms, scabies, diarrhea, and malaria were very common due to a lack of clean water and poor sanitary conditions. Grassroots healthcare was weak, and medicine was scarce. Illnesses were mainly treated with traditional herbal medicine and leaves.

🍚

Hunger: A constant obsession

Having meat for a meal was a luxury, only for Tet (Lunar New Year) or ancestors' death anniversaries. Rice mixed with sweet potatoes and cassava was an everyday occurrence. Many families suffered from prolonged starvation and frequent food shortages. Malnourished, stunted, and emaciated children were a common sight.

Nghe An Today

From those days of hardship, Nghe An has risen strongly. Roads, electricity, water, internet, hospitals, schools... everything has completely changed. But the resilient spirit, family bonds, and memories of the past remain forever in every child of Nghe.
